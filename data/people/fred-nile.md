Frederick John Nile (born 15 September 1934, Kings Cross) served as a Member of the NSW Legislative Council from September 1981 to March 2023 — a tenure of over 41 years, with a brief break in 2004 — representing the Call to Australia party, which he renamed the Christian Democratic Party in 1998. An ordained Christian minister, Nile was the most consistent and prominent legislative opponent of LGBTIQ rights in NSW across the entire period covered by this project.

During the years of peak anti-gay violence (mid-1980s to mid-1990s), Nile was an active presence in the NSW parliament. He opposed decriminalisation of male homosexual activity (1984), campaigned against the NSW AIDS awareness campaign (arguing it promoted homosexuality), attempted to ban the Sydney Gay and Lesbian Mardi Gras, and prayed publicly for rain on Mardi Gras day. He opposed Clover Moore's Homosexual Anti-Vilification Bill (eventually passed 1993), opposed equalisation of the age of consent (not achieved until 2003), and opposed marriage equality. His sustained legislative opposition contributed to the political environment in which hate crimes occurred and in which institutional responses were slow.

Nile was a member of the NSW Legislative Council Standing Committee on Social Issues that produced *Gay and Transgender hate crimes between 1970 and 2010* (Report 58, 2021) — the inquiry that directly recommended the judicial process that became the Sackar Inquiry. He appears in the committee's membership list as "Revd the Hon Fred Nile MLC."

Contextual note: Nile was born in Kings Cross — the epicentre of Sydney's gay and transgender community — and attended Cleveland Street High School, the same school later associated with the "Alexandria Eight," a gang of students documented by ACON as having carried out a series of anti-gay attacks in the 1980s. The connection is incidental (his school years preceded the gang by three decades) but reflects the deep entrenchment of these histories in the same streets and institutions.
