Witness A is a gay man who gave evidence to the NSW Parliamentary Committee inquiry (Report 58) under a suppressed name. He is a doctor.

In the early 1990s, he was dragged from a telephone box in front of his workplace and bashed, leaving him with facial injuries.

After escaping his attacker, Witness A went to the local police station to make a statement. He was laughed at and turned away. He was told that he did not know his attacker's name, and that the description he had given — height, ethnic appearance, hair colour, eye colour — was not comprehensive enough to warrant a search.

As a doctor, Witness A turned to his then-employer. They were equally unsupportive. They refused to take a statement and denied his request to take sick leave to recover from his injuries. Instead, Witness A was told to take leave without pay and not to come to work "for fear of people asking about his injuries." In response, he went to work anyway — with bruises on his face — and told people exactly what had happened.

Witness A paid for this. His workplace ignored him, threatened him, and attacked his character, credibility, and skills: "After all, you aren't a worthy person to support anyway." He was repeatedly told it was all in the past, that it "does not really affect you now," that he needed to "let it go" and stop being "so worried about it." His career was profoundly affected. He was not successful in applications for senior positions and eventually had to withdraw from his specialist training program because he did not feel safe or supported. Only years later did he re-enter the medical profession, when a gay physician encouraged him to complete his training at another institution.

Witness A described his experience with his former employer as a classic example of *gaslighting* — a denial that an incident occurred, followed by a belittling of his emotions and an undermining of his confidence in his own perceptions, resulting in "a complete delegitimisation of the situation."

He suffered post-traumatic stress disorder and was unable for many years to walk or drive down the road where he had been bashed. He chose not to report a subsequent assault by a gang of youths on himself and his boyfriend: "I had had my very negative experience. I was not going to have that repeated. I knew that nothing was going to happen." He no longer trusted the police, and no longer trusted his employer.

Giving evidence in November 2020, Witness A called for acknowledgement "from the top down," stating: "It cannot be just, 'It happened in the past and we are not part of it now,' because that is just the same thing all over again. It is just the same playbook being pulled out."

He expressed bitterness that his attack had happened in 1992 but was still being discussed in 2020.
