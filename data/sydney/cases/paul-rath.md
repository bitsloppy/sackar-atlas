Paul Edward Rath was twenty-seven years old when his body was found wedged between rocks at the base of the Shelley Headland cliffs in Manly on the morning of 16 June 1977 — barely twelve months after Mark Stewart died at the same location.

He was in a seated position, head between his knees. His trousers were down at mid-thigh. A set of rosary beads and one shoe lay nearby. In his coat pocket was a handwritten note, signed by him and written for the children he taught as a volunteer catechist:

*God loves little children. Children love God with your whole heart and whole soul. Let God's light shine upon you from day to day. Let your little hearts become a replica of His. Place your faith and love in his sacred heart. And he will find a special place in heaven where you will be with Him for eternity.*

The Sackar Inquiry found there is "objectively reason to suspect both that the death of Mr Rath was a homicide and that LGBTIQ bias was a factor." It recommended a fresh inquest. He is the only person in the North Head cluster for whom the Inquiry made a specific recommendation.

## Who was Paul Rath

Paul was born on 18 January 1950 in Sydney, the eldest of eight siblings in a close-knit Catholic family from Manly. His parents raised their eight children on Pittwater Road, and Paul was still living at home when he died.

He left school in fourth form, around age fifteen, following what the family described as a "nervous breakdown." He had been under the care of psychiatrist Dr Otto Reichard since 1971, diagnosed with schizophrenia and treated with antipsychotic medication — haloperidol and thioridazine at moderate doses. Dr Reichard's final note, written shortly before Paul's death, said he had been "fairly well" and showed "no special indication of any current suicidal tendencies."

Paul worked for a time at the "House with No Steps," a disability services organisation. He left because he found the conditions depressing and had been on a pension since. He did voluntary work he clearly valued: teaching religious education as a catechist at local Catholic primary schools, writing small pieces of prose for the children. He attended church regularly. He was known to walk the local area, including to the headland at Fairy Bower, to sit on the clifftops and relax.

His youngest brother Gregory told the Inquiry in 2023 that, approximately a year before Paul's death, Gregory asked Paul whether he was gay. Paul told him that he was, adding some remarks about a male friend from the "House with No Steps" that may have been a reference to sexual activity.

## The evening of 15 June 1977

At 4:30pm on Wednesday 15 June, Gregory briefly saw Paul at the family home. Paul was in his room playing records. Gregory's impression, he told the Inquiry, was that Paul "just wasn't right" — though at the time he told police and the Coroner that his brother was happy, because their mother had asked him to say so.

That evening, Paul visited his sister Helen Colman at her flat on Denison Street, Manly — his first visit. Helen, then 18, was living with a man the Inquiry called I266, a teacher about fifteen years her senior. Paul relayed a phone message I266 had left: he would be staying away an extra night and would not be home as expected. I266 had also mentioned wanting to meet Paul. Paul said he did not like or trust I266, and seemed puzzled by the suggestion.

When Paul left — after dark, in his suit — Helen asked where he was going. He said he was going to meet someone, but the person had asked him to keep it confidential.

The next morning, Helen learned he was dead.

When I266 returned, he asked Helen to say nothing about Paul's visit or his phone call, fearing exposure of their relationship would cost him his job. Helen told her mother; her mother said "no need to stir anything else up." The conversation that was Paul's last was not recorded for forty-six years.

## The investigation

At 7:20am on 16 June, fisherman Alfred Barrett found the body. Senior Constable Ross Parry attended and noted the scene was known to be "frequented by homosexuals." He arranged penile and anal swabs.

By the following morning — before the swab results had come back and before the post-mortem had been conducted — the OIC marked "NO SUSPICIOUS CIRCUMSTANCES" in capitals in the Report of Death to Coroner. The clothing Paul was wearing was returned to his family or destroyed on his mother's authority within days.

The penile swabs tested positive for semen. The clothing, which showed staining in scene photographs that Dr Linda Iles later found could not be fully explained by blood from a post-fall nose injury, is gone. No blood grouping was conducted. No DNA will ever be possible.

Helen Colman was not interviewed. Enquiries were not made at Paul's church. Residents near Bower Street were not canvassed. The family home — whose phone Paul had received a call on from I266 that day — was not checked.

Since at least 2013, the NSWPF and Strike Force Parrabell knew of Helen Colman's concerns about I266. They never contacted her. Parrabell made no reference to this lead in its BCIF or Case Summary.

## Parrabell and the SCOI

Strike Force Parrabell answered all ten BCIF indicators as "Insufficient Information" but classified the overall case as "No Evidence of Bias Crime." Sackar J found this inconsistency "points to the likelihood of classificatory confusion."

The Inquiry commissioned expert forensic pathology reports from Dr Linda Iles and a forensic science report from Jae Gerhard. Dr Iles found that if the clothing staining was blood, it could not be accounted for by blood flowing from Paul's nose after impact — it "accommodates a scenario" where injury occurred before his fall. Gerhard was unable to determine from black-and-white photographs whether the staining was blood or mud, meaning the question is permanently unresolvable.

## Sackar's finding and Recommendation 1

Sackar J's formal finding:

> *Mr Rath died on 15 or 16 June 1977 as a result of spinal injuries sustained in a fall from a height at North Head, the cause of which cannot be determined.*

He declined to adopt the Coroner's accidental finding. He found there is "objectively reason to suspect both that the death of Mr Rath was a homicide and that LGBTIQ bias was a factor."

**Recommendation 1:** The Commissioner of the NSWPF, or a serving police officer, should make an application for a fresh inquest into Paul Rath's death.
