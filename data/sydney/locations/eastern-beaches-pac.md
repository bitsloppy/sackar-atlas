The Eastern Beaches Police Area Command (PAC) covers Bondi Beach, Bondi Junction, Coogee, Randwick, Waverly, and the clifftop areas between Bondi and Tamarama. Within this jurisdiction lie Marks Park (Bondi) and the Bondi–Tamarama clifftops — some of the most significant locations in the documented history of anti-gay hate crimes in NSW.

**Historical significance — Marks Park and the Bondi cliffs:**

Marks Park, overlooking the sea at the Bondi–Tamarama headland, was a known beat throughout the 1980s and 1990s. ACON's *In Pursuit of Truth and Justice* (2018) specifically identifies Marks Park as a site where multiple anti-gay killings occurred, and where gangs — documented as the "Bondi Boys" — conducted repeated attacks on men attending the beat. ACON records cases of men found at the base of the Bondi–Tamarama cliffs, having been pushed or fallen while trying to escape attackers, in circumstances where blood was later found on the cliff edge and witnesses described seeing the attacks in progress.

**Ross Warren and John Russell:**

The most documented cases within this jurisdiction are those of television newsreader Ross Warren and barman John Russell, both of whom died on the Bondi–Tamarama cliffs in 1989. Police originally dismissed their deaths as accidental falls. In 2005, then Deputy State Coroner Jacqueline Milledge — working with Operation Taradale's reinvestigation led by Detective Steve Page — found that both had been murdered amid a spate of attacks on homosexual men at the clifftop beat. The original police investigations, conducted within the jurisdiction of what is now the Eastern Beaches PAC, were condemned by Coroner Milledge as "lacklustre" and "shameful." The Warren and Russell cases are among the clearest documented examples of the institutional failure to properly investigate gay hate crime deaths.

**Bondi Memorial:**

In recognition of the history documented partly within this PAC's jurisdiction, ACON and Waverley Council have developed the Bondi Memorial at Marks Park — a permanent public artwork ("Rise" by artist John Nicholson) honouring victims and survivors of LGBTIQ hate crimes. Waverley Council, whose local government area covers the Marks Park site, contributed $100,000 in capital works funding and additional asset management resources. The memorial was planned for construction from March 2021.
