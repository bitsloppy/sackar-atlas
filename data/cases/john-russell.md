John Russell was thirty-one years old when his body was found at the foot of the
Marks Park cliffs on the morning of 23 November 1989. He was lying on the rocks
with his head facing towards the cliff face and his feet towards the ocean — a
position forensic experts later described as unusual for an accidental fall. He
had strands of hair on his left hand that were not his own.

He had been at the Bondi Hotel the night before, celebrating with his best friend
I403 and talking happily about the future. He had just inherited money, was planning
to build a home on his father's property at Wollombi, and intended to travel
Australia. His father was driving down from Wollombi on the Friday to collect him.

He never made it.

The NSW Special Commission of Inquiry into LGBTIQ Hate Crimes (Sackar J, 2023)
examined his case as a Category A matter. Sackar J confirmed the 2005 finding of
Deputy State Coroner Milledge: John Russell was thrown from the cliff onto rocks by
a person or persons unknown, and there is objectively reason to suspect that LGBTIQ
bias was a factor in his death.

## Who was John Russell

John Alan Russell lived with his brother Peter in Bondi — they had lived together
all their lives. He worked as a barman at Bronte Bowling Club and one day a week at
a school in Double Bay. He was described by his best friend I403 as "happy go lucky"
and a social drinker who didn't touch drugs.

Peter Russell knew his brother was gay. He described John as not being "extremely
active in this area," and was not aware of him being in a relationship at the time.
I403 didn't think John was into using beats — but Peter Russell later told Operation
Taradale (2001) that John had referred to Marks Park by name, calling it "The Beat,"
and had been there on a couple of occasions to meet other men.

He was thirty-one years old. Everything was looking up.

## The night he died

On the evening of Wednesday 22 November 1989, John Russell was at home with his
brother Peter in Bondi. Peter described him as being in good spirits.

I403 met him there at about 6pm and they went to the Bondi Hotel together at around
7pm. They sat in the bar and talked about John's plans — the kit home, the
inheritance, the travel. I403 recalled they spoke about how there were "happy times
ahead." John had been buying the drinks because it was the day before I403's pay day.

I403 left at about 11pm. John stayed. He had a similar evening planned for Thursday
night with friends at the Legion Club in Charing Cross, and on Friday his father Ted
was driving down from Wollombi to collect him.

## What was found at Marks Park

John Russell's body was found at approximately 10am on Thursday 23 November 1989 on
the rocks at the base of the Marks Park cliffs. He was wearing a red sloppy joe,
faded jeans, gym boots and socks, and had $4.60 in loose coins and a red credit card
holder on him.

Two forensic pathologists — Dr Allan Cala and Professor Johan Duflou — examined
the position of his body. Both considered it unusual for an accidental fall: his
head was toward the cliff face and his feet toward the ocean, a position that
implied the body had twisted 180 degrees relative to a forward fall. Dr Cala said
he would not have been able to move at all following the fall.

The loose coins scattered around Russell's body may have been used to signal
availability at the beat — a documented practice at Marks Park.

Human hairs were observed on his left index finger. Peter Russell, who saw the hairs
at the morgue, said they were too long and the wrong colour to be John's. The hairs
were bagged for analysis, but had been lost before the first inquest on 2 July 1990.
No forensic analysis was ever performed on them.

A post-mortem examination on 29 November 1989 found extensive skull fractures,
bilateral lacerations, and fractures to both arms. Blood alcohol was 0.255g/100mL —
a level forensic expert Associate Professor Moynham described as causing "marked
intoxication," impaired balance, impaired coordination, and "a diminished capacity
to protect himself from danger."

## The police investigation

Police from Bondi Police Station attended the scene. The OIC was a junior plain
clothes constable — Constable Dunbar, later known as Sally Johnston — who concluded
the death was most likely "accidental" due to intoxication and did not pursue it as
a homicide. No persons of interest were identified.

The hairs from John's hand were lost before they could be analysed. His clothing was
eventually returned to police for use in a public appeal — displayed on a mannequin
in Bondi. When Peter Russell and his father Ted saw the mannequin, the clothes were
spotless and the shoes clean. They had been washed. Whatever biological evidence
the clothing might have held was gone.

The first inquest, held on 2 July 1990 — seven months after Russell's death, before
Deputy State Coroner Derek Hand at Glebe Coroners Court — returned an open finding:
he had died of multiple injuries from a fall, but whether accidentally or otherwise
was undetermined. No transcript of the inquest survives. By the time it was held,
the hairs from his hand had already been lost.

## Operation Taradale and the Milledge Inquest

Russell's case was not reinvestigated until 2000, when Operation Taradale — led by
Detective Sergeant Stephen Page — assembled the evidence on the three connected
Marks Park deaths. The Taradale work identified links between Russell's death and
other violent assaults on gay men in the area.

On 9 March 2005, Deputy State Coroner Milledge found that John Russell had been
thrown from the cliff onto rocks by a person or persons unknown — a direct homicide
finding, more specific than any prior finding. The NSW Commissioner of Police later
told the Sackar Inquiry that he agreed the Inquiry "could properly conclude that
Mr Russell's death was a gay hate crime."

## Strike Force Neiwand — and the secret reversal

Despite the 2005 homicide finding, no reinvestigation of Russell's case was
embarked upon until Strike Force Neiwand in 2015–2018.

The Sackar Inquiry found that Neiwand devoted significant effort to discrediting
Operation Taradale and pursuing theories of suicide or misadventure, rather than
homicide, in relation to all three deaths. In doing so, Neiwand internally overturned
Milledge's homicide finding for John Russell — and never told his family, and never
notified the Coroner. Sackar J found this to be "intellectually dishonest."

Peter Russell learned about the Neiwand reversal through the Sackar Inquiry.

## What Sackar found

> *"I find that John Alan Russell died at Marks Park near Bondi between 22 and 23
> November 1989. The cause of death is multiple injuries sustained when he was thrown
> from the cliff onto rocks by a person or persons unknown. In my view, on the
> evidence available to the Inquiry, there is objectively reason to suspect that
> LGBTIQ bias was a factor in the death."*

Strike Force Parrabell had categorised the death as "evidence to suspect a bias
crime." The Sackar Inquiry confirmed this. No one has ever been charged.
