Ross Warren was twenty-five years old when he disappeared from near Marks Park
in the early hours of 22 July 1989. He was a television presenter — the weather
reader on WIN4 Television in Wollongong, where he was well liked and described as
friendly, good-natured, and career driven. His body has never been found.

The NSW Special Commission of Inquiry into LGBTIQ Hate Crimes (Sackar J, 2023)
examined his case as a Category A matter — one of the deaths subject to full
public examination. Sackar J confirmed the 2005 finding of Deputy State Coroner
Milledge: Ross Warren was a victim of homicide by person or persons unknown.
There is, in the Inquiry's view, objectively reason to suspect that LGBTIQ bias
was a factor in his death.

## Who was Ross Warren

Ross Bradley Warren lived and worked in Wollongong, where he had a flat of his
own and a job he was good at. He was known at WIN4 as an extremely friendly and
good-natured person, with a wicked sense of humour. He visited friends in Sydney
approximately every second weekend, staying with Craig Ellis and Paul Saucis at
their home in Redfern.

His family — his parents and siblings — knew he was gay. So did his employer.
Among his friends, at least some knew that he sometimes visited the Marks Park
beat. His close friend Craig Ellis had himself met Warren there in 1988. In 1985,
Warren had been arrested at a beat and convicted of gross indecency; he was placed
on two years' probation.

## The night he disappeared

On the evening of Friday 21 July 1989, Warren finished the weather at WIN4's 6pm
news and drove to Sydney. He arrived at Craig Ellis and Paul Saucis's home in
Redfern between 8pm and 9pm.

At around 10:30pm he left and drove to Oxford Street in Darlinghurst, where he met
a WIN4 colleague, Phillip Rossini. Together they visited several bars and
nightclubs along the Oxford Street strip. They parted ways at about 2am on
Saturday 22 July 1989. Rossini last saw Warren driving east on Oxford Street,
towards Paddington. Warren was wearing black shoes, blue faded jeans, a white
turtleneck shirt, and a black sports coat.

On Sunday afternoon, when Warren did not turn up for work, Ellis and Saucis went
to Paddington Police Station and reported him missing.

## What was found at Marks Park

Ellis and Saucis then searched Marks Park themselves. That Sunday evening, they
found Warren's car parked on the corner of Kenneth Street and Marks Lane, very
close to the park. They reported this to police.

The next morning — Monday 24 July — they returned. Ellis walked down an access
route to the rock shelf below the Marks Park cliff and found Warren's keys placed
in a "pocket" formed in the rock, near the water's edge. They reported this too.

No photographs were taken of Warren's car, or of his keys, or of the scene. His
body has never been found.

## The police investigation — and its failure

The officer in charge at Paddington Police was Detective Sergeant Kenneth Bowditch.

On 28 July 1989 — just six days after Warren was reported missing — Bowditch
wrote in the occurrence pad that police had "no such view" that Warren had been
murdered, and were of the opinion he had "fallen into the ocean in some manner."
The "investigation" effectively finished then. It had lasted four days.

Coroner Milledge later described this as "a grossly inadequate and shameful
investigation. Indeed, to characterise it as an 'investigation' is to give it a
label it does not deserve."

What made it worse was that Bowditch had lied about the steps he'd taken. When
interviewed years later as part of Operation Taradale, he claimed to have:
submitted a brief to the Coroner (the Coroners Court had no record of receiving
it); sent documents to the Missing Persons Unit (who had no such documents); taken
statements from Ellis and Saucis (both said he never did); involved the Air Wing
and Water Police (neither had any record of involvement); and obtained Warren's
health and dental records and recent photographs (none were ever found).

The NSWPF accepted in submissions that Bowditch's "apparently false assertions"
were "extraordinary" and that his failures amounted to "a total failure to respond
appropriately to the disappearance of Mr Warren."

Warren's disappearance was never reported to the Coroner as required.

## Operation Taradale and the Milledge Inquest

In the late 1990s, Warren's mother wrote repeatedly to police seeking some
finality. Her letters eventually reached Detective Sergeant Stephen Page in 2000,
whose enquiries led to the formation of Operation Taradale.

Operation Taradale brought together the evidence on Warren, John Russell — whose
body had been found at the base of the Marks Park cliff in November 1989 — and
Gilles Mattaini, who had disappeared near Bondi Beach in 1985. The investigation's
work formed the basis of the Milledge Inquest.

On 9 March 2005, Deputy State Coroner Milledge delivered her findings. On Ross
Warren: *"I find that Ross Bradley Warren died in Sydney on or about 22 July 1989.
Whilst the cause and manner of death are unknown, I am satisfied that the deceased
was a victim of homicide perpetrated by person or persons unknown."*

Milledge also found that the Marks Park beat was known at the time to be a
location where gay men were targeted and thrown — or threatened to be thrown — from
the cliffs, and that this MO strongly supported the probability that Warren,
Mattaini, and Russell all died this way.

## Strike Force Neiwand

Marks Park was a known beat and known crime scene. Despite Milledge's 2005
findings, no reinvestigation of Warren's case was embarked upon until Strike Force
Neiwand in 2015–2018.

The Sackar Inquiry found that Neiwand devoted significant effort to discrediting
the findings of Operation Taradale and to pursuing theories of suicide or
misadventure — rather than homicide — in relation to each of the three Taradale
deaths. No new charges were ever laid.

In June 2015, NSWPF announced a reward of $100,000 for information leading to the
conviction of person/s responsible for the disappearance and suspected deaths of
Ross Warren and Gilles Mattaini, and for the death of John Russell.

## What Sackar found

Justice Sackar, examining all three Taradale deaths together, confirmed the
Milledge finding:

*"I find that Ross Bradley Warren died in Sydney on or about 22 July 1989. Whilst
the cause and manner of death are unknown, I am satisfied that the deceased was a
victim of homicide perpetrated by person or persons unknown. In my view, on the
evidence available to the Inquiry, there is objectively reason to suspect that
LGBTIQ bias was a factor in the death."*

No one has ever been charged with Ross Warren's murder. His body has never been
found. His mother wrote letters to police for years seeking answers. No
accountability has followed.
