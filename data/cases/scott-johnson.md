Scott Johnson was twenty-seven years old when his body was found at the foot of
the cliffs at Blue Fish Point, North Head, on the morning of 10 December 1988.
He was naked. A folded pile of his clothes and personal effects was found at the
top of the cliff. He had suffered unsurvivable traumatic injuries.

He was an American citizen, completing a PhD in mathematics. The Australian
National University posthumously awarded him his doctorate in 1995.

His case took thirty-five years to reach a conviction.

## Who was Scott Johnson

Scott Russell Johnson came from the United States to study mathematics in Australia.
He was twenty-seven years old, completing doctoral research, and living in Canberra.
He had a partner at the time of his death. He was gay.

He went to North Head on a day in early December 1988. He was found the following
morning at the foot of the cliffs at Blue Fish Point — a location that was, from at
least the 1970s, a well-known gay beat. Police in 1989 said they did not know it
was a beat. Evidence before three subsequent coronial inquests showed this was not
true.

## What was found at Blue Fish Point

Johnson's body was found on the morning of 10 December 1988 at the base of the
cliffs at Blue Fish Point. He had suffered "unsurvivable traumatic injuries." He
was naked. A folded pile of his clothing and personal effects had been left at the
top of the cliff — an arrangement the first coroner took as evidence of planned
suicide.

The first inquest, held just three months after his death, on 16 March 1989, found
suicide. Deputy State Coroner Hand concluded Johnson "jumped from the top to the
rocks below with the intention of taking his own life." This finding rested on:
his partner's evidence that Johnson had once mentioned considering suicide after
thinking he might have contracted HIV; his personality being described as reserved
and introverted; and the absence of evidence of a struggle at the top of the cliff.

At that inquest, the OIC of the original investigation, DS Doreen Cruickshank,
gave evidence that the area was not known to police as a beat — based solely on
the absence of reported violence there. This evidence was incorrect. Multiple
people who used the beat in the late 1980s later gave evidence that violence *had*
occurred there and had simply gone unreported to police.

## Three inquests — thirty years

The 1989 suicide finding stood for more than two decades. Johnson's brother Steve
Johnson spent years — at his own expense, with investigators he retained himself —
gathering evidence and pressing for a reinvestigation.

In 2006, Steve Johnson drew the attention of former Detective Sergeant Stephen Page
(who had led Operation Taradale into the Marks Park deaths) to the similarities
between Scott's case and the Bondi cliff deaths. Page agreed: the cases were too
similar to ignore.

On 27 June 2012 — twenty-three years after Scott's death — a second inquest was
held. Deputy State Coroner Forbes made an open finding, departing from the suicide
conclusion because evidence about the Bondi gay hate killings had "sown a seed of
doubt."

On 30 November 2017 — twenty-nine years after the death — State Coroner Michael
Barnes delivered the third inquest finding. His conclusion was unambiguous: Johnson
had fallen from the clifftop at Blue Fish Point "as a result of actual or threatened
violence by unidentified persons who attacked him because they perceived him to be
homosexual." Barnes heard evidence from users of the North Head beat in the late
1980s who reported suffering or witnessing violence there. He found it was unlikely
either misadventure or suicide had caused the death.

## Strike Force Macnamir and the resistance to a homicide finding

Between 2013 and 2017, the NSW Police Unsolved Homicide Team conducted a major
reinvestigation: Strike Force Macnamir, led by Detective Chief Inspector Pamela Young.

Macnamir conducted extensive work. But the Sackar Inquiry found that Young
persistently and strongly held the view that Scott Johnson's death was a suicide —
and framed the prospect of a homicide finding as a "defeat" for NSW Police. Text
messages and coronial statements showed she and Homicide Commander Michael Willing
actively resisted what they called "letting the Johnsons win."

In the "Opinion" section of Young's coronial statement — provided ahead of the
third inquest — she argued the suicide theory in terms that State Coroner Barnes
subsequently rejected in detail. Barnes' finding contradicted Young's view on
almost every contested point.

Sackar J found it "remarkable and unfortunate" that such senior officers in the
UHT exhibited this resistance to the possibility of a homicide, and described
their attitude as consistent with the institutional defensiveness documented across
the report.

## The conviction of Scott White

After the 2017 homicide finding, Strike Force Welsford was established to
investigate. On 12 May 2020, Scott White was arrested and charged with the murder
of Scott Johnson.

White pleaded guilty to a charge of manslaughter in February 2023. He was sentenced
in June 2023. The case is *R v White* [2023] NSWSC 611. In sentencing, Beech-Jones CJ
at CL described the suggestion that Scott Johnson had died by suicide as "an absurd
suggestion."

## What Sackar found

The Sackar Inquiry examined the Scott Johnson case through the lens of Strike Force
Macnamir's institutional failures, in Chapter 11 of Volume 3. The Inquiry accepted
State Coroner Barnes' 2017 finding: Scott Johnson was killed by persons who
perceived him to be homosexual.

The Inquiry's broader finding was about the systemic resistance that had delayed
this conclusion for almost thirty years — from the inadequate 1989 investigation,
through the UHT's "nil priority" classification in 2012, through Strike Force
Macnamir's dogged resistance to a homicide finding, to the ultimate conviction
in 2023. Each stage added time to a case that, in the Inquiry's view, should have
been investigated as a potential hate crime from the beginning.

Scott Johnson was awarded his PhD in mathematics by the Australian National
University in 1995 — seven years after he died.
