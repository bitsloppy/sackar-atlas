Gilles Mattaini was twenty-six years old when he went for a walk near Bondi Beach
on 15 September 1985 and never came back. He was French, living in Sydney with his
long-term partner Jacques Musy. His body has never been found.

Nobody reported his disappearance to the police in 1985. There was no investigation.
The first time police took a formal interest in what happened to Gilles Mattaini was
fifteen years later, in 2000, when Operation Taradale drew connections between his
disappearance and the deaths of Ross Warren and John Russell — three gay men, all
in or near the same stretch of coastal cliff between Bondi and Tamarama.

The NSW Special Commission of Inquiry into LGBTIQ Hate Crimes (Sackar J, 2023)
examined his case as a Category A matter. Sackar J could not confirm the manner of
death — the evidence is simply not there. But he found, objectively, reason to
suspect both that Mattaini was murdered, and that LGBTIQ bias was a factor.

## Who was Gilles Mattaini

Gilles Jacques Mattaini was born in France on 25 October 1958. He met Jacques Musy
in Paris in about 1978; they were together for seven years. In 1983, they moved to
Sydney together, Musy first, Mattaini following.

By September 1985 they were living in an apartment in Ramsgate Avenue, Bondi Beach.
Gilles worked casually as a barman at the Menzies Hotel near Wynyard, where their
friend Marc Hubert was his supervisor. He was described by Musy and his friends as
a shy and private person, someone who enjoyed long walks along the coastal path
from Bondi to Bronte, listening to music through his headphones.

He was not, by all accounts, a user of the Marks Park beat.

His visa had expired, which was causing him some concern. He felt, as Hubert put it,
"trapped" — he couldn't leave Australia and come back. But he was, according to
Musy's weekly phone calls from France, laughing and happy. He was buying things for
the apartment. A friend from France, Antony Wyszynski, was coming to visit. He was
looking forward to Musy's return.

## The day he disappeared

On 16 August 1985, Jacques Musy flew to France to see his family. He was still there
when Gilles went missing.

On 15 September 1985, Gilles Mattaini went for a walk. A witness known as "Terry"
saw him that morning in the Bondi area. He did not show up for his shift at the
Menzies Hotel the following day.

When their friends realised he was missing, they did what they could to find him.
They had no success. Vincent Ottaviani — a friend and colleague — was supposed to
have reported the disappearance to police. He believed he had. No record of any such
report has ever been found. Ottaviani died in 1991.

When Musy returned from France, he found Gilles' keys, headphones, and a yellow
spray jacket missing from the apartment. Everything else was in order.

Gilles Mattaini's body has never been found.

## No investigation — and why that matters

There was no police investigation in 1985. No crime scene. No witness statements
taken while memories were fresh. No search organised. No forensic evidence gathered.

This is not an unusual feature of this case — it is, as the Sackar Inquiry made
visible, the characteristic feature of this era. Gay men who were attacked, who
disappeared, or whose bodies were found in circumstances suggesting violence, were
systematically under-investigated. Their communities often didn't report incidents
to police precisely because they didn't expect police to take them seriously. That
expectation was, repeatedly, correct.

In Mattaini's case, the complete absence of a 1985 investigation means that
everything the Inquiry later knew about him — his character, his movements, his
state of mind, the possible circumstances of his death — came from statements taken
fifteen or twenty years after the fact. The limits of what the Inquiry could conclude
flow directly from what was never done in 1985.

## Operation Taradale and the Milledge Inquest

Operation Taradale, led by Detective Sergeant Stephen Page from 2000, was the first
police investigation into Mattaini's disappearance — fifteen years after the fact.
It brought together the Mattaini case with the disappearance of Ross Warren (July
1989) and the death of John Russell (November 1989). All three cases shared a
geography, a pattern, and a silence.

Taradale gathered statements from Musy, from friends, from colleagues. It assembled
the brief that went before Deputy State Coroner Milledge.

On 9 March 2005, Milledge delivered her finding on Mattaini:

> *"I find that Giles [sic] Jacques Mattaini died on or about the 15 September 1985
> in Sydney. The cause and manner of his death remain undetermined as the evidence
> before me does not enable me to say."*

Milledge noted that the persons of interest linked to the deaths of Warren and
Russell "would have been far too young at the time of Mr Mattaini's disappearance."
She found she could not make a homicide finding. But she also noted "a strong
possibility that he died in similar circumstances to the other men."

## The question of his death

In the years after 2005, attempts were made to obtain a DNA reference sample from
Mattaini's mother, Renée, in France — in case his body was ever recovered. These
attempts failed. Renée Mattaini died in March 2017 and was cremated. A sister,
Liliane Michel, was not contacted by Strike Force Neiwand for a DNA sample.

Marine scientist Professor Robert Brander, commissioned by the Inquiry, gave evidence
that a large wave event occurring between 25 and 28 July 1989 — just days after
Ross Warren's disappearance — would have transported a body on the water's surface
hundreds of metres offshore and into deeper ocean currents. The same analysis would
apply to Mattaini's disappearance in September 1985, a period when such wave events
are common off the Sydney coast. This is likely the reason neither body was recovered.

Sackar J found the Inquiry could identify objectively reason to suspect that Mattaini
was murdered, and that the possibility of his death at the hands of LGBTIQ hate
assailants is, in Sackar's words, "inescapable." He walked regularly along the
coastal path that passed Marks Park — a well-known beat, and a known target area
for anti-gay violence. He was a gay man living in Bondi in the 1980s, when gay men
in the area were targeted and thrown from those exact cliffs.

## What Sackar found

> *"I find that Gilles Jacques Mattaini died on or about 15 September 1985 in
> Sydney. The cause and manner of his death remain undetermined. In my view, on
> the evidence available to the Inquiry, there is objectively reason to suspect both
> that the death of Mr Mattaini was a homicide, and that LGBTIQ bias was a factor
> in the death."*

This is the most that can honestly be said. The evidence that was never gathered in
1985 cannot be recovered now. The body has never been found. No one has ever been
charged.

Jacques Musy gave a statement to Operation Taradale in 2002 — seventeen years after
the man he had shared his life with since 1978 walked out the door and did not
come back. The Inquiry noted that Renée Mattaini, Gilles' mother, died in 2017
still not knowing what had happened to her son.
